My Experience with TransferWise Borderless Accounts

Jan 30, 2018

When buying things online, you probably use a credit or debit card. It’s fast and convenient. But when making larger purchases, it makes sense to look at all available options.

I live in Romania, which has an obscure currency (RON). That means I have to pay a sizable conversion fee any time I want to buy something from abroad.

Most Romanian banks have a foreing exchange spread of at least 3%, which means I’m paying a 1.5% fee each way. For sums larger than €10.000, you can negotiate a better transaction rate, but for smaller sums you’re out of luck (unless you have very high volume).

Besides the conversion fee, banks also charge a transaction fee of 0.1% or more. This fee can’t be lowered, at least not for mere mortals.

The Promise of Cheap International Transfers

TransferWise guarantees a total fee of exactly 1% for RON transfers, making it cheaper than using most banks. That was reason enough for me to give it a try.

At first, TransferWise was just another money transfer service, similar to Western Union or Moneygram - you could send funds to a specific person, but not to a specific bank account. You can ask a family member to go pick up the cash, but you can hardly ask online shops or business partners to do the same thing.

Recently, however, they fixed this problem by introducing a feature called a “borderless account”. This is just a collection of regular bank accounts in various countries, managed in one place.

Setting up a TransferWise account

Day 1

Signing up for an account is a breeze. Before I can make any transfers, I have to upload two documents: a proof of identity and a proof of address.

I already had a picture of my national identity card, so I use that as proof of identity. For proving my address, I just generat a statement from my local bank and upload it.

Day 2

My account is approved. I activated the currencies I’d be using most often, namely RON, USD and EUR.

Making my first transfer

I transfer RON into my TransferWise account from my domestic bank. It has a trivial fee (5 RON).

Day 3

Convert RON to USD - As promised, the fee is 1%, minus the fees for getting funds in and out of TransferWise.

Initiate USD transfer to a bank in the United States.

Day 4

Check up on the transfer status and notice the site is only partially loading. Turns out they recently did a redesign and were having some trouble with their servers.

Day 5

Get an email saying my transfer bounced back. It turned out that their UI silently “corrects” bank account numbers that contain letters.

The only option the UI presented was to fix the recipient details. Given that the account I was trying to send funds to wasn’t acceptable to the system, I had to call Transferwise support to get them to cancel the transfer.

Giving it another go

Day 6

The funds show up in my borderless account again.

I do another conversion, from USD to EUR, incurring another 1% fee.

I initiate a transfer from my EUR balance to another EUR account within SEPA.

Day 7

I see the funds in the destination account.

Conclusion

I had the bad luck of conducting my experiment during a period when the website was experiencing some technical difficulties. Assuming this only happens once in a blue moon, I’m not going to hold that against them.

I am however dissapointed that transfers to virtual accounts don’t work. At the very least, I would have expected my transfer request to be rejected immediately, instead of the UI silently changing the transfer details and then having to wait 2 days for the transfer to bounce back.

Other than that, TransferWise seems like a good service: transparent fees, friendly user interface and decent phone support.
